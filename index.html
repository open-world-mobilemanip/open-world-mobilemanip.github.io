Abstract

Robots in the open-ended real world such as homes has been a long-standing research problem. However, in current research, robots are often studied in closed-off lab settings. We instead study the problem of mobile manipulation in the open world with a full-stack approach. We find there are many issues. First, mobile platforms are often extremely expensive and not powerful, too large, and not reliable enough for such open-world tasks. We design a small and agile mobile manipulation system that can be easily assembled with an off-the-shelf 6-DOF robotic arm and an omnidirectional wheeled base with a total cost of under 20,000 USD. Secondly, we choose a task: articulated objects for its ubiquity and usefulness in everyday robots. We collect a large teleop dataset of diverse real-world doors, drawers, and cabinets with total of 20 real articulated objects across 4 different buildings. However, we find it is impossible that robots can be successfully zero-shot on every door in the diversity of open-world environments. Instead, our robot is designed to continually learn from its diverse real-world experiences. We find that our robot is able to open many doors and articulate many objects in the real-world with less than one hour of real-world trials. We plan to open-source all the components to allow the community to build upon these results toward making progress on this challenging problem.



Online Adaptation.

We start by initializing our policy using a limited set of training data through behavior cloning. The central challenge we face is operating with new objects that fall outside the training domain. To address this, we've developed a system capable of fully autonomous Reinforcement Learning (RL) adaptation, utilizing a Vision-Language Model (VLM) for reward determination. In a trial period of 30 minutes, this system demonstrated significant improvement, boosting the success rate from 25% to 70% without any human intervention.
